Primary Recommendation: Bilateral Laminectomy L3-L4 and L4-L5 WITHOUT Fusion

Based on the MRI findings showing stenosis-predominant pathology with ligamentum flavum hypertrophy, facet arthropathy, and canal narrowing (but NO large disc herniations), the optimal treatment is decompressive laminectomy without fusion.

Why Decompression Alone is Optimal:

  • Targets the actual pathology: Removes thickened ligaments and bone compressing the nerves

  • Lower risk: No hardware complications, pseudarthrosis, or adjacent segment disease from fusion
  • Faster recovery: 6-8 weeks vs 4-6 months for fusion
  • Proven effectiveness: 80-90% success rate for stenosis without instability

üí° Key Clinical Pearls for Stenosis Surgery:

1. Stenosis ‚â† Instability: Most patients with spinal stenosis do NOT have instability. The correlation between stenosis and instability is actually quite low (~15-20%). Don't assume fusion is needed just because stenosis is present.

2. The "Fusion Paradox": Studies show that for stenosis without instability, adding fusion to decompression provides NO additional benefit for leg pain (the primary symptom) but increases complications, cost, and recovery time.

3. Motion Preservation Matters: Given this patient's prior L5-S1 laminectomy (which may have caused some stiffness), preserving motion at L3-L4 and L4-L5 is particularly important for maintaining functional mobility.

4. Adequate Decompression is Key: The surgeon must remove enough bone and ligament to truly decompress the canal - timid decompression leads to persistent symptoms. However, this can be done while preserving enough facet joint to maintain stability.

5. Get Flexion-Extension Films First: This is the KEY imaging study that determines if fusion is truly needed. If these show <4mm translation and no angular instability, fusion is NOT indicated.
